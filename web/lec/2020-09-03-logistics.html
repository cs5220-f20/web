Welcome to CS 5220, Applications of Parallel Computers, the Fall 2020 deluxe edition. I am your host, David Bindel. This slide deck covers some of the logistical aspects of the course. It is a strange semester, so the logistics are complex. I have taught at Cornell for the past decade, but I have not had a semester like this before, either. Also, this is a one-man band; I have no course staff this semester. So, please: bear with me! If you want to start with an overview and introduction to the technical part of the course, feel free to back out and look at that slide deck instead. It should be hyperlinked from the same course schedule page that brought you here. But the logistics are important, too, so I urge you to go through this deck carefully and ask questions, either on Piazza or in the course meeting.

Enrollment

  • Initial enrollment was restricted to non-CIS majors
  • Plenty of room for the waitlist
  • Plenty of room generally, and open add/drop
  • Please do not register for a formal audit
For several years, we have restricted enrollment in upper-division CS classes. This is to make sure that our students, particularly the MEng students (who register late and who are only hear for one year), can take the classes they need. But parallel scientific computing is not ML, and we are not in danger of running out of room. If you were on the waitlist, you should be automatically enrolled in the next couple days. And if you want to add the class now, the add/drop period is not restricted. You can enroll through September 16, though sooner is better. In some semesters, I allow students to formally audit 5220. This semester, if you want to just follow along, I recommend keeping up with the material on the web page. Otherwise, I expect you to participate in the homework, the group projects, and other course activities.

Course community

  • A class is not just the lectures and HW!
  • We want group interaction
  • This can be synchronous or asynchronous
  • Several things planned, will adapt as we go
A class is never just about lectures and homework. Particularly in a class like 5220, you should learn as much from your peers as you learn from me. Managing that peer-to-peer learning is going to be a special challenge this semester, but I have some plans for how we can foster it, whether through synchronous meetings or asynchronous online platforms. I have a lot of ideas about this, and will spend a few moments now telling you about them. I am as new at this as you are, so I will take any good ideas you might have, and we can also always throw out things that are not working!

Course community

The typical mix of 5220 students includes:

  • CS undergrad and MEng students (and a few PhD)
  • PhD students from other disciplines

This means we assume you will:

  • Know a C family languages.
  • Not know some background.
  • Be a teacher and a learner.
Let's start with who you are. Usually, the two main audiences for the class are CS undergraduates and MEng students, and PhD students from other disciplines who need to do parallel computing for their research. There are also always a few CS PhD students, non-traditional students, or undergraduates from other disciplines. I assume you know how to program in some C family language, and I assume you know enough to learn how to navigate in a Unix environment without too much lecturing from me -- though I will provide some references for those of you who haven't done this much. But otherwise I try not to assume much about what you already know. What this means is that I expect some of you to be confused when I talk about computer architecture, and some of you to be confused when I talk about fluid dynamics, and some of you to be confused when I talk about numerical methods. And I may say some things that confuse everyone! Fortunately, you also have a lot of collective knowledge, and students in this class have worked together to do some pretty remarkable things. There are at least two PhD theses in the past few years that I know arose largely from projects in this class! So plan to learn from each other and teach each other as much as you learn from me (and teach me). And be prepared to ask lots of questions.

Course meeting

  • Main meeting: TR 8:00-9:30
  • I will help coordinate alternate meetings, too
  • This is not about lecture (class is flipped)
  • Goal is discussion and small group activities
  • We may also do some programming together!
  • Zoom recordings posted to Canvas
The main meeting for the class is Tuesdays and Thursdays at 8:00 AM, Eastern. This is a very awkward time for students who are coming in from California, or even for Ithaca students who happen to be night owls. So we will coordinate alternate meeting times, too, though these may involve only students and not me. I call this a meeting rather than a lecture because I will not be going through the narrated lecture slides with you, for the most part. Instead, we will use the time for question-and-answer, discussion, and small group activities. We may also walk through code or tool demos together, partly so that you can ask questions as we go. The Zoom recordings of these meetings will be posted to Canvas, though any activity that happens in breakout rooms will not be recorded.

Course buddies

  • Role: Make sure nobody falls through cracks
  • One or two buddies per student
  • You can nominate, if you know people
  • I may also do some buddy assignments
Once we have a reasonable idea of who is taking the course, I will be making a list of "course buddies." A buddy is just there to check in and make sure that things are going OK. There will be reminders to check in with your buddies periodically through the semester. If you already know a couple people in the semester who you want to buddy with, I can take that into account. But it is also fine if you don't know anybody! I can help with buddy assignments.

Project groups

  • Most course work is done in small groups
  • We will assign groups for initial projects
  • Final project is at your discretion
  • Active collaboration and pair programming are encouraged!
Most of the work in this class is done in small groups, usually mixing people from different backgrounds. These are not the same as your class buddies. I will assign groups for the projects that are done in common, but you will have some choice about the final project group, just as you have a choice about the final project topic. I'd prefer that groups don't try to just split projects into a bunch of small pieces that can be put together at the end. The goal is to get you to work together. Online can actually be a good environment to practice collaboration techniques like pair programming, for example.

Collaboration and AI

  • 5220 is supposed to be collaborative!
  • Want you to use online resources effectively
  • But for academic integrity: cite!
This is supposed to be a collaborative class. There are no exams, and the only homeworks are there to make sure that you know the basics. There are also lots of resources online, and I want you to understand how things work well enough to be able to look things up, judge the answers you find online, and incorporate any good ideas into your own work. There is not a lot of room for cheating in all of this, but you definitely should cite any resources that you use -- whether in the form of help from a friend or from StackOverflow.

Finally, let's talk about the class information systems. The non-restricted part of the home page is all built from a public Github repository, and you should feel free to file issues or pull requests (or just send email) if I make mistakes! I will use our home-grown course management system (CMS) for homework submission and keeping track of grades. I also encourage you to explore for yourself; for example, I know that VisualStudio has support for pair programming, though I haven't tried it out myself. If you come across tools that you find useful, let us all know! Finally, we will be using various external computing resources over the course of the class, and I'll distribute information on those when possible.
